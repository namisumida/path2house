COLLEGE

Overall, half of all House members attended a public university/college for undergrad, while a third attended a private college and about one-in-ten (11%) an elite school. And while a vast majority received a Bachelor's degree, a small portion (6%) have not.

Some party differences emerge when it comes to undergraduate school type. House members who are Democrats are more likely to have attended private and elite schools (by 11 and 9 percentage-points, respectively) and less likely to have attended public schools (by 17 points).

GRADUATE SCHOOL

As expected, a majority of all members (69%) have continued on to higher education. Roughly a third have obtained a law (37%) or master's degree (34%), while very few have received a medical degree (5%) or PhD (4%).

Members who are Democrats are more likely to have gone to law school (by 12 points) and obtained a masters (by 10 points), but Republican members are more likely to have gone to medical school (by 6 points).

Interestingly, some of the few without Bachelor's degrees have obtained degrees of higher-education: Two have a law degree and another a medical degree. Some other impressive overlaps emerge: Several of those who received a master's degree also received a law and/or medical degree, and one member, Henry Cuellar from Texas, attended law school and received a masters and a PhD.

CAREER

When it comes to careers, there's a much wider range of experiences. Careers in business and private law are particularly popular (37% and 29%, respectively), as well as experiences in the military, education, and at non-profit organizations - each previously pursued by over 10% of House members. Other occupations are very rare, like religious leaders (1%) and sports-related jobs (2%).

Nearly all members (92%) have spent some time in one of these careers, with many - 46% - having worked in two or more.

When comparing by party, Republican members are more likely to have been in the military (by 16 percentage-points) and worked in a business/management-related job (by 21 points). Democratic members, on the other hand, are more likely to have worked in the non-profit (by 17 points) or education sector (by 13 points).

Newer members - those who joined in 2019 - have notable differences as well. Based on percentages, they are more likely to have worked in business (by 13 points) or non-profit organizations (by 11 points).

POLITICAL OFFICE

A majority of members (76%) have held a political office in the past. About half (46%) have served in state legislative roles and roughly a third (32%) in local government. Federal or state-level offices and public lawyers or judges each comprise of 14%.

But while most have held a political office in the past, only about a quarter (26%) have held multiple. Past experiences in state legislature and local government are popular combos, with 16% having held offices in both; among those who have held local government offices, about half (49%) have also had prior state legislative roles.

Roughly a quarter (24%) have previously not held political roles, and new members are more likely to be the case (by 18 points).
